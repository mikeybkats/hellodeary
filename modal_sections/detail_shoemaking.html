How to make a pair of shoes.

The photo above is from November of 2015. I was one of a group that studied shoemaking at a workshop in Port Townsend, Washington. As a lifelong craftsman, it was probably one of the most gratifying experiences I've ever had. Shoe making is an incredibly analog and ancient process. Before taking the workshop, it was utterly mysterious to me, and now I realize how truly utterly mysterious it is. The photos in this post come from several projects and serve to illustrate the overall process of shoe making.

Like all design work it begins with a simple sketch. Shoes are made on lasts - a mold representing the human foot, which the leather is stretched around to make the form of the shoe. The pattern is drawn directly onto masking tape that's adhered to the last. The drawing on the tape is cut around with a scalpel knife and transfered to a flat sheet of paper. This is the basic technique of patterning a shoe.

This closeup image depicts the level of detail required for patterning. If the lines drawn onto the last don't look good, then neither will the shoe!

Above are all the tools required for patterning: A scalpel, scratch awl, pencil and eraser, masking tape, paper, and of course the shoe lasts. It's also a good idea to have some samples of leather around to help inform the design direction.

After the shoes are patterned the pattern pieces are laid onto the leather. Every part of the hide has a slightly different strength, grain structure and thickness. Because of this, the shoemaker needs to be careful how the pieces are placed and cut.

aaaaaa While the uppers are being cut and sewn the toe caps and heel counters will be wet molded around the shoe last. Heel counters and toe caps afford the shoe structural integrity, attractive appearance, and extra level of protection to the wearer.

Next the leather midsole is attached to the bottom of the last with nails. A glue line is drawn around the perimeter and adhesive is applied liberally.

The leather upper is stretched around the last and tacked into place.

After the inner layer of leather is stretched around the last the toe caps and heel counters are glued into place.

The heel counters and toe caps are then sanded down so there is a smooth transition between surfaces. In this photo the toe caps are made from a synthetic material.

The leather uppers are stretched around the lasts one final time, tacked and glued in place. Then the out soles are glued on.

The shoes are pulled off the lasts. The edges of the soles are sanded down, and the leather is polished.

The last step is trying them on. Finished shoes. Now it's time to take them for a test spin around the block.
